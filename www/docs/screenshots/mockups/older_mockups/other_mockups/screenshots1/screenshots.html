While Docuverse is both a non-commercial and non-profit project, we are currently developing it "under cover". This is for several reasons. One, we do not want to announce Docuverse, gain a torrent of hype, and then having nothing ready for two years. Users would end-up reading about Docuverse in an article, and then come to a web-site that has nothing substantial. This is an issue which has plagued the Freenet, Gnutella, and Mozilla projects, which announced their existence before they had a substantial, working code base. It is better to secretly develop Docuverse until it is ready to be released in beta form, and then announce its existence. Further, Docuverse is possibly threatening to existing entrenched interests in the computer field, and keeping it secret until it is ready to be released gives these organizations less time to respond, kill off, or clone Docuverse.

 

The Docuverse Manifesto

The web has become a stagnant play-thing of corporations and inaccessible international organizations. Its original promise of bringing democratic, grassroots speech from everyone to everyone has fallen by the way-side. Now is not the time to give up, dismissing the web as an idealistic dream. Instead, we can dream into existence a new evocative vision, a new web that rests on democratic principles.

Docuverse is a non-commercial, non-profit project to do just this. We are creating a new space, named the Docuverse, which envelops the existing World Wide Web and moves it forward in new innovative, democratic directions. We are replacing and collapsing existing web browsers, web servers, web editors, and office productivity suites into a single, unified new program named Docuverse. Since software is also law, similar to how a building's architecture changes how people can interact, Docuverse is also a socialtecture. In this new web socialtecture everyone has a voice, regardless of wealth, power, or technical skill, and people can collaborate to create spontaneous, organic spaces for politics, business, and fun. It is easy to comment on the powerful, and the web becomes human-centered. It is a profoundly democratic, decentralized vision.

The 10 Dreams of Docuverse

The engine of Docuverse is a next-generation web site known as a DocuBoard. At their most basic DocuBoards are simply super-charged web-sites that make it possible to create, maintain, share, and discuss documents accessible only to the people you want. Underlying DocuBoards, however, are ten dreams that are the heart of the Docuverse:

Dream 1: Freedom of Speech must include affordable access to freedom of speech.

Imagine living in a kingdom that gave everyone the right to criticize the royalty without fear of arrest or punishment. Also imagine, though, that in order to open your mouth to make your criticisms you had to pay thousands or hundreds of thousands of dollars. Would you say this society truly has freedom of speech?

This is the situation in most countries today that claim they have freedom of speech. To reach thousands of people by radio, you have to purchase a hundred thousand U.S. dollar radio license. To reach an entire community, you have to own a million dollar newspaper printing press. Basically, the only way to communicate is to become a business; sorry, a rich business.

The web was supposed to change this; the web was supposed to make it possible to reach millions of people, even if you aren't a company. The reality is different. One of the tragedies of the current World Wide Web is independent, non-commercial web-sites being forced to commercialize in order to communicate with millions of people. I call this the Tragedy of the Dot Commons. Imagine you are a hobbyist poet who creates art after you come home from work; you share your material with people on your web-site out of love, not out of money. At first your web-site either doesn't cost anything or costs maybe ten dollars a month. However, if you are successful, and people begin to flock to your web-site, you could find yourself paying literally thousands of U.S. dollars a month for powerful servers, hosting, and fast server connections. As an artist of course you want to reach millions of people, so you change your .org to a .com and attempt to become a business. Unfortunately, we've learned that the Internet is not the best place to create businesses, so you go out of business, and your art disappears from cyberspace.

Docuverse changes this. Instead of having a web site stored on a single centralized server that takes money and technical knowledge to run, DocuBoards are broken into pieces and redundantly stored across each of the member's and visitor's personal computers. When a user types in a DocuBoard address into the Docuverse program, Docuverse spontaneously re-assembles the DocuBoard and gives it to the user transparently; now this user is himself free to hand out pieces of the DocuBoard to those who request it. The upshot of this is that the more user's who access a given DocuBoard, the more it is redundantly spread across member's computers, and the better its performance and accessibility becomes. This type of system is known as peer-to-peer, and is similar to the way in which music-sharing programs like Napster, Gnutella, and AudoGalaxy work. The fundamental effect of this is that no intervening corporation needs to be paid to run a server or buy expensive server bandwidth from, allowing non-commercial sites to potentially reach thousands of people without the Tragedy of the Dot Commons from occurring.

Dream 2: Web sites should be organic, grassroots, and bottom-up

Docuverse includes everything needed to create sophisticated, grassroots DocuBoards. First, Docuverse includes wizards and templates to get you started creating a diverse range of DocuBoards, from business oriented intranets, to personal weblog diaries, to online political newspapers. Docuverse even includes editors to create business-oriented documents, such as purchase orders, presentations, spreadsheets, and drawings. Second, membership in your new DocuBoard can be restricted to individual friends, family-members, or person's in your company, or extended to the entire world. Finally, every page on your new DocuBoard can be edited right through the Docuverse browser by pressing the Edit button if the member has the necessary privileges.

Docuverse opens up a world of opportunities. For example, several people can collaboratively create an online DocuBoard investigative journalism newspaper and post articles all completely through the Docuverse browser. A company could create a DocuBoard intranet, and let company employees create arbitrary DocuAreas within this intranet, such as a company-wide accessible employee weblog, employee created subprojects, or employee written newspapers, all without top-down authority. This feature, coupled with the fact that DocuBoards are distributed across many members computers and not stored on a server that suffers from scarcity, should make many innovative, bottom-up collaborative web-sites possible.

Dream 3: The web should bloom with discussion

In the Docuverse browser is a single button, titled Comments, that holds a great deal of power. Every web site and web page, including older World Wide Web sites and DocuBoards, automatically have a discussion board attached, no questions asked. For example, go to www.nike.com using Docuverse, click the Comment button, and a pane pops open with a discussion area on it. Individual pages also have discussion boards automatically attached as well, so readers can comment on specific articles or pages.

Docuverse strikes a balance between the complete chaos of allowing everyone in the world to comment on a given page and the complete control of only allowing members to comment -- it gives everyone the best of both worlds. Users can choose to view only comments posted by the members of that DocuBoard or can choose to see non-members comments as well.

Docuverse also includes another important form of discussion; with a click of a button Parody sites can be created. By selecting Parody from a pull-down menu, the entire contents of a web-site will be copied and replicated into a new parody DocuBoard. However, this web-site will prominently feature that it is a parody and not the original, that you are unaffiliated with the original, and pointers to the original site. You can then add members just like any DocuBoard, and collaboratively create something new from the old. For example, you can automatically parody the New York Times to create a comedic, artistic, or political work, or maybe just create a juvenile piece of graffiti. As we all know, sometimes the best art is generated from old art, even if American copyright law forgot that.

Dream 4: It should be easy to find discussion commenting on the powerful

Imagine another fantasy society, similar to the freedom of speech one, where everyone has the right to criticize the powerful. However, imagine that those criticisms have to be inscribed on a needle, and the needle is thrown into a haystack. Voices affirming that society and the powerful, though, get to have their messages written on giant billboards that can't fail to be seen. If you can't easily or readily find dissenting voices in a society, does that civilization really make it possible to comment on the powerful?

The current World Wide Web works the same way. The powerful own the radio stations and the billboards lining our highways; it's easy for us to find their message. What about the dissenters, Socratic voices on the edges? It's hard to find their message, to investigate the alternatives, almost as difficult as finding a needle in a haystack.

Docuverse changes this. Imagine being able to type www.nike.com.comment.labor into your browser, and instantly finding out Nike's labor policy. Or how about typing the address www.microsoft.com.comment.antitrust, and seeing comments about Microsoft's antitrust record. Maybe you want to type www.greanpeace.org.comment.disagree and see the perspective of those who offer flaws in the environmental movement?

Docuverse changes web site names in a new way. Every web site name can now have a .comment ending attached to it, followed by a comment topic, such as .environment, .religion, .agree, etc. There are about fifty predefined .comment endings. After typing one of these after any domain name, such as www.whitehouse.gov.comment.money, the Comment panel will open with all comments that have been posted by users while viewing www.whitehouse.gov that concern money.

These .comment filters are auto generated by the users who post comments. When a user posts a comment they can choose to assign it to one of the fifty pre-defined categories, such as environment, religion, etc with a simple pull-down.

User's can also click the What's Related button to see what's related to this web-site across all the different categories beyond the simple white-washed recommendations Alexa makes.

Dream 5: Everyone should be able to communicate in complex ways

Sure, nowadays anyone can create a simple home-page or a basic collection of web pages. However, can they create an advanced intranet, a complex online newspaper, or a rolling updated weblog diary? Not unless they either have strong technical skills or the money to afford sophisticated server-side content management systems. All of the tools above make it possible for ordinary, non-technical Average Joes to create extremely powerful online communities with a few mouse-clicks.

Dream 6: Names are power - and no one should own that power

Domain names, such as www.acme.com or www.myschool.edu, are part of a semi-centralized network known as the Domain Name System (DNS). DNS is currently controlled by an unaccountable, undemocratic organization that has tended to kowtow to large organizations and companies, and which has maintained and artificial scarcity in domain names as bad as the radio spectrum. Docuverse banishes these bastards (we did a study and decided they are no longer needed) by completely decentralizing the DNS system in a peer-to-peer fashion in exactly the same way as DocuBoards are broken into pieces amongst those who use the DocuBoards. Just as breaking DocuBoards into pieces made it possible to create DocuBoards for free, breaking the DNS system into pieces shared amongst those who use it make it possible to use domain names for free. No longer will you have to pull out your credit card, register a domain name in a difficult process, and wait a few days till it activates. Instead, naming your DocuBoard becomes as easy and transparent as naming a file on your local hard-drive; you don't even know it's an issue.

Domain names can also now have spaces in them, and Docuverse further explodes the DNS name space with hundreds of new domain endings. The venerable .com, .edu, and .org endings are now joined by .religion, .fan, .sucks, .cooperative, .government, .religion.falun gong, .politics.conservative, .country.america, and more. Your world isn't just about .COMpanies, .EDUcational institutions, or .ORGanizations, so why should the web?

Dream 7: Web sites should have standardized user-interfaces for navigation and provide more navigation tools

Application programs, such as Adobe Photoshop or Macromedia Dreamweaver, currently have standardized user-interfaces. They all have pull-down menus, most have toolbars, and most follow similar conventions. This allows new users to quickly switch between different programs and quickly learn how to use them. Web sites, though, have a confusing disarray of user-interfaces. Menus are in completely different areas on different web-sites, if they exist at all. Some are on the left, some are on top, some are in frames, and some are even fun nifty Java applets! While novelty is the spice of life, having to learn a different user-interface for every web-site is a pain.

Docuverse standardizes the navigation user-interface across all DocuBoards, and introduces a substantial number of new ones. First, all DocuBoards now have a navigator panel on the left of their screen that shows an outline of all pages and areas on that web-site; user's can easily click around to quickly jump to any page on the site. Second, all DocuBoards display a 'cookie trail' that communicates to users how deep within a DocuBoard they currently are. Third, the standard back and forward buttons in browsers gain two new friends: an Up button and a Front button. The Up button takes you one step up in the hierarchy of the DocuBoard, while the Front button will take you to the front page of that DocuBoard. Fourth, all DocuBoards now include an Index button, which allows users to navigate a DocuBoard in the same way they can turn to the index in the back of a book. Fifth, all DocuBoards have a standard search field that is always in the same place across all DocuBoards, so users can easily use Search to find information on that DocuBoard. Sixth, all DocuBoards have a What's New button that will show user's what has recently changed on that DocuBoard so user's can come up to speed. And finally, all DocuBoards have a Members button so user's can easily find out the members of a DocuBoard and communicate with them.

These features should make it significantly easier for users to do what they do most on the web: find the information they are looking for, without having to relearn how to do this at each web-site.

Dream 8: Users should control their information

Industry players, from Sun to Microsoft, are currently bandying around ideas to store your personal account information on centralized servers controlled by them or other unaccountable organizations. While this will make it possible to create single sign-on services for web-sites, it will be at the expense of bolstering current monopolies, loss of privacy, and loss of control over your personal information.

Docuverse makes single sign-on between DocuBoards possible without giving up control of your personal information. Instead of storing your personal information on some foreign server controlled by someone else, all your information is stored on your local computer. When you attempt to access a DocuBoard, that DocuBoard will ask your computer for the information it needs to log you in, such as your username. A prompt will appear informing you that the foreign DocuBoard would like this information. You can now choose to not allow the foreign web-site to have this information; you are in control.

Credit card information works the same way. This will be stored on your own machine, not on some foreign server. In Docuverse you will never have to type this information in for a DocuBoard; however, if a DocuBoard wants this information, the Docuverse browser will protect you and tell you that the DocuBoard wants this information. Again, you can choose to deny this information.

Dream 9: The web should be smart enough to be a database

Docuverse includes two powerful features known as Smart Sites and Smart Templates, which are an implementation of Tim Berner Lee's Semantic Web. Docuverse comes bundled with a wide variety of pre-created areas for your DocuBoard for commercial, political, non-commercial, and just plain fun communication. One of the bonuses of having users create their DocuBoards through pre-created templates is that the browser can now record exact metadata on what everything actually is so that computers can understand the information. For example, when you create an online newspaper area on your DocuBoard, Docuverse knows what items on the board are newspaper articles, what are bylines, what are headlines, etc. This information is used in powerful ways, such as in the automatic creation of the site Index.

Smart Templates are similar to Smart Sites in that the Docuverse browser saves information about every field with the document as metadata. Since Docuverse comes bundled with a large number of page templates, such as Purchase Orders, News Articles, To Do Lists, etc., what these fields mean can be standardized across them. For example, Smart Templates always know what fields are addresses or customer names, across every template. It knows that a particular person authored a page, or that someone parodied a page. In this way knowledge across the entire Docuverse is represented in the same way and can be accessed by intelligent computers. We have turned the web into a giant intelligent database, taking it to the next level.

Dream 10: Everything should be wrapped into one tool but accessible by other tools

As you can see from the dreams above, Docuverse collapse what used to be many different systems into a single program. Gone are web servers, stand-alone web browsers, content management systems, web editors, DNS servers, authentication servers, and office productivity suites. The Docuverse is more accessible because ordinary users now don't need to learn a multiplicity of programs or configure complicated servers.

Docuverse, though, is open to other developers creating tools that interoperate with it, though. First, it is completely open-source, so other users can take its code-base and graft on new additions. Further, DocuBoards are saved in an open XML file-format, replacing HTML and proprietary Microsoft Word files, so other's can modify their software to load and edit DocuBoard pages. For example, Macromedia could modify Fireworks to load your Docuverse purchase order, or Microsoft could add filters to Microsoft Office to load your Docuverse presentation.

Supporting Technologies

Docuverse is an open-source project, and will be released under the GPL (GNU Public License). If you are not familiar with what open-source means, it means that users have the freedom to see the program's source code, modify it, and distribute the program and modifications free to other users; users are also protected from other users making proprietary, private modifications. In this way the pool of openly available code constantly grows.

Docuverse heavily depends on other open-source projects in order to make it a feasible system to build and maintain. The following projects will be used in the code base:

  • OpenOffice - OpenOffice is a full, cross-platform, open-source clone of Microsoft Office. It includes a full word-processor, presentation, drawing, and spreadsheet package. It is completely Microsoft Office-compatible, and saves files in an open, XML-based file format that is well-documented. Docuverse will heavily rely on OpenOffice's technical infrastructure and editors; in many ways Docuverse can be imagined as a 'shell' that wraps OpenOffice. Docuverse will also rely on OpenOffice's cross-platform component infrastructure named UNO, Universal Network Objects. UNO allows full-featured Office-like applications to be written in C++, Java, and a variety of scripting languages.
  • JXTA - JXTA is an open-source, peer-to-peer library written in Java. It abstracts away the difficulties of the actual, underlying peer-to-peer protocol being used, making it possible to optimize the Docuverse sharing algorithm in the future without changing the Docuverse code base. Further, JXTA makes it easy to add new peer-to-peer network services in the future.
  • JXTA Search - JXTA Search is a peer-to-peer service for JXTA originally created by a startup company, purchased by Sun, and released as open-source. JXTA Search includes a full, distributed, meta-data based search service.
  • Java - Much of Docuverse will be coded in Java, in order to take advantage of Java's rapid development time and extensive code libraries. Note however that Java's Swing and AWT packages will not be used due to performance, memory, and functionality issues.
  • SWT - SWT, the Simple Widget Toolkit, is an open-source GUI toolkit for Java written by IBM as part of their Eclipse project. SWT is a very thin, cross-platform GUI toolkit that has significant advantages over Java's Swing and AWT GUI toolkits. It has extremely low memory usage, very good performance, fast startup times, and adapts to the native theme set by the user.
  • Mozilla - Mozilla is a cross-platform, open-source browser and browser engine that are extremely standards compliant. Docuverse will use the browser engine to render pre-Docuverse web-pages. The Mozilla browser engine does not suffer from the same performance and memory issues that plague the Mozilla browser itself, since it does not include the XML GUI infrastructure that the Mozilla 'chrome' is built from.

 

Docuverse Timeline

There is no exact timeline for release of Docuverse; it is estimated that the full project will take a year and a half to complete. Docuverse is an unusual open-source project in that the bulk of the time will actually be spent in usability testing and honing the user-interface. In addition, Docuverse will try to find talented graphic designers and advertisers to create a brand identity through professional icon design, a logo, slogan, targeted message, etc. Here are the stages of development:

  1. Usability Testing - Guerilla usability testing done with full mockups of the user-interface. Full functionality of the application is also determined. This phase will be finished the end of March 2002. Its deliverable will be a Software Engineering Requirements Document that will be unusual; instead of a traditional, technical Requirements Document it will be crafted as the help documentation for the user of the application, complete with screenshots. It will be tailored to fit the style of the existing OpenOffice help documentation.
  2. Docuverse Identity Package - This task will be done by a third party while usability and architectural work is occurring. The deliverable for this is a new name for Docuverse and DocuBoards (these are temporary names), a slogan, a logo, and a focused re-write of the Docuverse Manifesto/Overview that is appropriate as the introduction screen when Docuverse is first started up. This introduction screen should 'sell' the idea of Docuverse and explain how it works from an end-user perspective so the user can get started.
  3. Graphic Design of User-Interface - This task will be done by a third party while usability and architectural work is occurring. The deliverable for this is a unified look-and-feel and 'visual message' based on a color-scheme, icons, layout and graphic design of the pages generated by the template wizards, wizard graphics, and more.
  4. Security Analyses - Identify the potential motivations to destroy or incapacitate the Docuverse network, possible security issues in the existing open-source code bases being used, and steps that should be taken to minimize or erase these risks. This task will be done simultaneously with the Architecture step. The deliverable for this is a Security Analysis paper.
  5. Architecture - Create the actual blueprints and system architecture for Docuverse. The deliverable for this will be a System Architecture document.
  6. Programming - Actually create the Docuverse system. Outside volunteer programmers will probably be privately approached to help build the system. The deliverable for this step is a feature-complete beta-quality system, with possibly some features dropped for future releases due to time-constraints or complexity-issues. This release will be considered a 1.0 beta 1 release.
  7. Docuverse Web Site Creation - Create the web-site that will be used by developers and users to get and develop Docuverse once Docuverse is announced.
  8. Announcement and Beta Release - Get outside advertising and public advocacy-oriented individuals to volunteer and help determine the best way to announce and promote Docuverse, and do an actual beta release.
  9. Bug Testing - Build a bug-testing community and perform successive releases of higher and higher quality. New functionality will not be accepted; however minor usability changes can be made for the first few beta releases. Each successive release will be called 1.0 beta 2, 1.0 beta 3, etc., until 1.0 Release Candidate 1 and 1.0 Release Candidate 2 are ready. The final release of Docuverse will be called Docuverse 1.0.
