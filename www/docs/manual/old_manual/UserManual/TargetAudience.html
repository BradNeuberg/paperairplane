Target Audience

Paper Airplane is focused on meeting the needs of Intermediate Computer Users.

Intermediate computer user's are defined as:

  • People who use email and surf the web, but:
    • Don't use PGP to sign their emails or understand the PGP Web of Trust
    • Don't know how to configure an SMTP email server
  • They predominantly use Microsoft Word for document creation. They also use Microsoft Word to create web pages, but are dissatisfied with it's HTML output and difficulty in transfering as email attachments. However, they:
    • Don't have a graphic design background
    • Don't use sophisticated web creation tools such as Macromedia Dreamweaver
  • They have a beginners understanding of what peer to peer is after using or hearing about Napster, but:
    • Don't feel comfortable with the existing crop of peer to peer file storage applications (as opposed to peer to peer file sharing applications) due to complicated user-interfaces. They are most likely not even aware of the existence of these applications, such as Mnet, The Circle, Chord, etc.
  • They are sophisticated in a creative and political sense, but have only recently begun to understand how they can transfer this side into use on the Internet.
  • They might be beginning to grasp how powerful Internet communities can be, and have some interest in establishing one for their own particular interests.
  • They enjoy their computers enough, and do enough file sharing using applications such as LimeWire or Kazaa, that they have a broadband connection
  • They use Windows and have a suitably powerful computer
  • They understand the following words or concepts: the web, web page, URL, web address, web-site, word processor, document, web browser, save, create, collaboration, online communities.
  • They don't set up web servers or use HTML.
  • They can purchase a domain name online, but hosting it and entering the values needed to configure it is difficult and annoying.
  • They prefer integration and ease of use over power

Intermediate computer users should feel liberated by Paper Airplane and prefer it over the competition: setting up a traditional web server, using Microsoft Word and distributing Word files using email attachments, setting up a Content Management System or groupware solution, etc.

The following types of users are not the targets of Paper Airplane; they would either feel constrained by Paper Airplane or confused:

  • Naive users
  • Sophisticated Users
  • Professional Graphic Designers
  • Computer Gurus

Each of these types of users is defined below.

A Naive User is someone who predominantly uses AOL, if they even go online. They are afraid of computers. They don't use Microsoft Word, and don't know what a word processor is. They don't even know what a browser is. When they touch a mouse they handle it like it is a delicate piece of machinery that will break at the slightest slip. If they are forced to use a computer, they attempt to minimize the amount of time they spend on it.

A Sophisticated User has technical or semi-technical training. They are comfortable setting up their own web-servers. They may have begun playing around with server-side scripting, such as PHP or ASP, but definently haven't done server-side object-oriented programming using languages such as Java. They have basic to intermediate system administration skills, such as configuring a DNS server or editing the scripts needed for Apache. They enjoy tinkering around and being unconstrained by a graphical user-interface.

A Professional Graphic Designer wants to maximize the aesthetic or interactive value of their web pages and web sites versus communication and editability. They use powerful tools, such as Adobe Photoshop and Macromedia Dreamweaver. They expect maximum control over presentation, and have a large amount of experience in designing and creating web sites and web pages. They are visual individuals versus verbal individuals. They are relatively satisfied with the existing web, but would balk at using the Paper Airplane editor since it promotes discursive, verbal sites over visual ones and doesn't support the HTML tricks they are used to.

A Computer Guru is anyone above a sophisticated user. They might be professional server-side Java programmers, extremely knowledgeable Linux system administrators and users, etc. They are comfortable with the existing architecture of the web as it suits their needs and knowledge well. They are comfortable with powerful but complex user-interfaces, scriptability, and configurability. They expect maximum flexibility, preferably without a GUI. They also understand existing security concepts, such as public-key crytography, webs of trust, certificates, revocation, etc.
